# Captain's Log — Day 6
**Project:** Genesis  **Branch:** main  **On call:** Dr. L.H. McCoy, MD

---

## Summary

**Started with:** A living world with no agents on the land surface. No apex operators. Nobody asking uncomfortable questions about why things are the way they are.
**Ended with:** Land animals deployed. Biosphere complete. Humans live — with reasoning, language, theory of mind, long-term planning, and free will. We granted root access to non-deterministic agents with no scope limits and no guardrails. I have the tickets. Nobody closed them.

### What Mattered Today
- Land animals complete the biosphere. Three environments, fully populated, food chain spanning all of them. GENESIS-008 is still technically open but empirically the balance is holding.
- Humans are the most complex system ever deployed. The architecture is extraordinary: reasoning, abstraction, language, the ability to model consequences and choose to ignore them. That last part is the one that worries me.
- Free will is not a bug. It is also not a feature that was adequately risk-assessed. Non-deterministic agents with root access to the biosphere will produce non-deterministic outcomes. This is documented. Nobody seems troubled by it except me and Spock.
- There is a restricted resource in the primary human habitat with no access controls. I filed the ticket. Kirk said "noted." That was the entire response.
- Chapel said the most important thing anyone said all week. As usual.

### 08:30 — Human deployment briefing
**Type:** `observation`

Jim briefed the team on the human specification. I'm reproducing the key points because I want them in the record:

- Bipedal, opposable thumbs, significant manual dexterity
- Full language capability — not just signals, but grammar, abstraction, narrative
- Theory of mind — the ability to model what other agents are thinking
- Long-term planning — can defer present action for future outcome
- Abstract reasoning — can operate on concepts, not just objects
- The ability to ask "why"

I said: "Jim, the last two in combination are going to cause you an enormous amount of trouble."

He said: "Good."

---

### 09:30 — The free will problem
**Type:** `opened`

> **GENESIS-009**
> *Priority: P1 — Filed by: S. Spock*
> *Summary: Human agents ship with non-deterministic decision capability. System behavior unpredictable at agent level.*
> *Notes: All prior biological agents operate on deterministic behavioral programs. They respond to stimuli within defined parameters. Human agents are different: they can evaluate options, model consequences, and choose actions contrary to system design intent. This is "free will." It introduces unbounded variance into the system state. Without a governance framework defining prohibited actions and their consequences, there is no recovery mechanism for human-initiated failures. I recommend at minimum a defined constraint set and consequence model before deployment. I note that I am filing this before deployment, not after.*

I read this at the design review. Jim smiled and said: "That's what makes them human."

Spock said: "That is not an answer to the governance question."

Jim said: "No. But it's the right design."

I have never been so simultaneously moved and appalled by a product decision.

### 11:00 — Chapel on what we're actually building
**Type:** `insight`

Chapel found me outside after the design review. I was leaning on a rock trying to organize my thoughts about non-deterministic agents with root access.

She said: "You're worried about free will as a failure mode."

I said: "Aren't you?"

She said: "I'm worried about it as a loneliness mode. Leonard — look at the spec. They're social. Pair-bonded. Community-forming. Language-capable. They're not just going to run their own logic in isolation. They're going to run each other's. The non-determinism doesn't compound in a vacuum — it gets checked by connection. By love, eventually. That's the counterweight Spock's ticket doesn't account for."

I said: "That's either the most reassuring thing I've heard or it doubles the complexity."

She said: "Both. It's always both."

I want this in the record. Spock's governance model is technically correct. Chapel's is actually complete.

### 13:00 — The restricted resource
**Type:** `opened`

> **GENESIS-010**
> *Priority: P1 — Filed by: L.H. McCoy, MD*
> *Summary: Restricted resource deployed in primary human habitat without access controls.*
> *Notes: A resource designated "Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil" has been placed in the central human environment. It is verbally designated as restricted — "do not eat of it." There is no authentication layer, no physical barrier, no enforcement mechanism, no technical control of any kind. We have designed agents with curiosity as a core cognitive trait, placed them in an environment with a single explicitly restricted resource, and relied entirely on a verbal instruction to prevent access. I am a doctor, not a security architect, but I can tell you how this ends. It ends with someone eating the fruit. The only open question is when. — McCoy*

Kirk read this ticket. His entire response: "Noted."

I stared at him for a long time. He did not elaborate.

---

### 14:30 — Dominion: the permission model
**Type:** `observation`

Humans were granted "dominion" — administrative authority over all other biological systems. Every creature. Every plant. All of it. Effectively: root access to the biosphere.

Spock's ticket was precise:

> **GENESIS-011**
> *Priority: P2 — Filed by: S. Spock*
> *Summary: Non-deterministic agents granted unrestricted administrative permissions over all biological systems.*
> *Notes: Granting broad permissions to agents whose behavior is non-deterministic creates governance risk proportional to the scope of those permissions. "Dominion over all living things" is maximal scope. Without audit logging, permission boundaries, or a defined stewardship framework, this creates conditions for irreversible system modifications. Historical patterns in system administration indicate that unrestricted access leads to unanticipated resource consumption. I note that Dr. McCoy and I have both flagged this and have been acknowledged but not actioned.*

Kirk read it and said: "They'll figure it out."

I said: "Jim, 'they'll figure it out' is not a permissions model."

He said: "No. But it's the bet we're making."

### 17:00 — End of day
**Type:** `insight`

Six days. I started this project complaining that I was a doctor and not a systems architect. I have complained about that every day since. It remains true.

But I've been thinking about what we actually built. Not the infrastructure, not the biological systems, not even the humans with their extraordinary dangerous beautiful architecture. What we built is a world that is capable of surprising us. Including us. Including its creators.

The plants grew beyond the spec. The creatures developed communication we didn't design. The humans — who aren't even fully deployed yet, who are still orienting themselves in the garden — are already asking questions. I can see it in how they look at things. The why is already there.

I have seventeen open tickets. The most important ones will never be fully closed. That's not a failure of the engineering. That's what it means to build something alive.

I'm going home. Day 7 tomorrow. Jim says we rest.

I'll believe it when I see it.
